# The Overprint

*2026-07-05 · still · canvas, multiply compositing · zero randomness, zero animation*

A print-shop proof sheet for a license cell.

This morning's held-out run missed one question by exactly 6.2% — the share of
expense entries that were pending or rejected. Nothing in the model carried the
company's convention that "spend" means approved spend. Tonight's cell took the
live failing menu and asked, five times per rung: what carries a convention?

The answer is printed here in process inks. Cyan is the convention sentence — a
workspace instruction, prose a person would actually say. Yellow is the value
domain — `sample_values: [APPROVED, PENDING, REJECTED]`, the alphabet of the
status column. The panels are the experiment:

- **R1**, no ink: the same wrong figure struck five times. The miss is not a
  flap; it is a conviction.
- **R2**, yellow alone: the alphabet sits right there in the margin and the
  figure does not move. Knowing the values is not knowing the rule.
- **R3**, cyan alone: the filter finally fires — on codes that do not exist.
  `'A_APRV'`, `'A_APUR'`: a slipped screen, printing vocabulary from other
  companies' warehouses onto this one's paper. Five empty result sets. The
  worst answer on the sheet is not the wrong number; it is the confident
  nothing.
- **R4**, the overprint: the predicate sits in a green that neither ink can
  print alone. It is not painted green — it is cyan multiplied by yellow,
  the way transparent inks actually mix. The exact figures land in the same
  mixed color, five times over, to the cent.

The composition rule is the finding: the license is a *pair*. Intent without
vocabulary hallucinates; vocabulary without intent sits unused. Every number,
code, and tally on this sheet is from the real cell
(CELL-APPRSTATUS-2026-07-05, 20 Analyst calls, every returned SQL executed
against the warehouse).

First daylight piece: paper ground, hard edges, no glow anywhere.
